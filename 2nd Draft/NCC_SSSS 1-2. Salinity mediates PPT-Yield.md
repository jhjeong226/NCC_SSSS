Salinity constitutes a critical intermediary that re-establishes the coupling between hydrological dynamics and rice productivity in coastal irrigated paddy systems. As a key environmental stressor, salinity inhibits crop growth and reduces yield through osmotic stress, nutrient imbalance, and ion toxicity, with its severity varying as a function of the timing and duration of exposure. In coastal paddy systems, precipitation deficits simultaneously reduce freshwater inflow to irrigation reservoirs and lower reservoir water levels, diminishing the dilution capacity against reservoir salinity while facilitating seawater intrusion into the coastal region [ref]. Seawater intrusion consequently elevates reservoir salinity, establishing precipitation as the primary modulator of salinity stress in coastal irrigated paddy fields. Hydrological dynamics thus govern rice productivity indirectly through their regulation of reservoir salinity, re-establishing the hydroclimatic coupling that irrigation infrastructure otherwise attenuates. Despite extensive investigation of salinity effects on rice physiology and yield under controlled experimental conditions [ref], the quantitative role of salinity in mediating the relationship between hydrological dynamics and rice productivity under actual irrigated field conditions remains insufficiently characterized.
