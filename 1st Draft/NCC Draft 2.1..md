# Recent Relationship between Precipitation, Salinity, and Rice Yield: Field Observations

Despite adequate water supply in irrigated rice paddies, precipitation remains a crucial factor influencing rice yield. The heatmaps in [[NCC Fig. 1]] show detailed correlation coefficients between precipitation and salinity on rice yield of two cultivars, Golden Queen (GQ) and Dongjinchal (DJ). Fig. 1-a demonstrates that the impact of precipitation on rice yield fluctuates based on its timing. During the early growing season, both cultivars exhibit a statistically significant positive correlation between precipitation and rice yield, with the strongest relationship observed in April (_r_ = 0.84, _p_ < 0.001). As the growing phase progressed, the correlation between precipitation and yield was switched from positive to negative. While early-season precipitation promotes rice yield, precipitation during the reproductive and ripening phases leads to lower productivity. The most significant negative correlation was observed with the precipitation accumulated in August and September for those cultivars (_r_ = -0.71, _p_ < 0.01).

In addition to precipitation, reservoir salinity also exhibited a significant relationship with rice yield, with its impact varying depending on the timing of exposure. Fig. 1-b illustrates that salinity initially negatively correlated with rice yield in May, which subsequently transitioned to a positive correlation in August. From 2018 to 2023, the most negative correlation between salinity and rice yield for both cultivars was observed in May (_r_ = -0.70, _p_ < 0.05), when the average reservoir salinity reached 1,674 _ppm_. In contrast, the most positive correlation was found in August (_r_ = 0.69, _p_ < 0.05), with average salinity levels of 870 _ppm_.

Precipitation plays a pivotal role in modulating reservoir salinity, with cumulative rainfall exhibiting a strong inverse relationship with salinity levels. Fig. 1-c compares the seasonal variations of 5-month cumulative precipitation and reservoir salinity, which are closely aligned. The scatter plots in Fig. 1-d further confirm the strong negative correlation between reservoir salinity and cumulative precipitation. Linear regression analysis of the seasonality of 5-month cumulative precipitation and monthly salinity highlights a notable agreement, with a correlation coefficient of _r_ = 0.98, underscoring the impact of rainfall on the coastal agricultural environment. Notably, the cumulative precipitation period with the highest correlation with salinity at a given month reflects the time required for sufficient freshwater input to dilute reservoir salinity effectively. This explains the time lag that appears as a pattern difference between the heatmaps in Fig. 1-a and Fig. 1-b.
