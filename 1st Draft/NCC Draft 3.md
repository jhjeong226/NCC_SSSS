Our study demonstrates that precipitation significantly affects rice yield even in well-irrigated conditions through the indirect influence on the cultivation environment. Previous studies primarily examined the isolated effects of individual climate variables, yet the comprehensive understanding of their direct and indirect interactions under field conditions remains limited. Our field observations from 2018 to 2023 revealed that spring precipitation positively influences rice yield during early growth stages (Fig. 1). In contrast, precipitation during reproductive and ripening phases adversely affects productivity (Fig. 1 and 2). Additionally, we observed an intensifying relationship among precipitation, reservoir salinity, and rice yield in recent years compared to historical trends, highlighting the changing impact of climate factors on coastal agricultural systems (Fig. 3).

Although irrigation infrastructure supplies sufficient water without precipitation, the timing and amount of precipitation remain critical due to indirect effects on rice cultivation environments. Since rice is susceptible to salt stress during its early vegetative stages 17,37, a clear negative correlation between early-season reservoir salinity and rice yield observed in Fig. 1-b is reasonable. Given the strong dependence of reservoir salinity on cumulative precipitation, the indirect influence of precipitation in April before transplanting, as observed in Fig. 1-a, is explainable. Adequate spring precipitation can contribute to yield by diluting reservoir salinity levels at this sensitive phase38. Particularly, rice yield was significantly reduced when salinity exceeded a specific threshold, 1,552 _ppm_, in our data (Fig. 3-c). Therefore, during the reproductive and ripening stages when salinity remains low, rice yield is more likely affected by precipitation-related factors such as lodging, reduced sunlight, and disease, rather than by salinity itself 39–41.

The contribution rate of climatic factors on rice yield has significantly changed over time, with spring precipitation and early-season salinity becoming increasingly important. From Fig. 1-d and Fig. 2-d, the seasonal connection between precipitation and salinity from January to June has recently become stronger. It suggests that the role of spring precipitation in regulating reservoir salinity has recently strengthened. Temporal correlation analysis also reveals a shift in correlation patterns, with spring precipitation and early-season salinity becoming more influential in recent years. This temporal change demonstrates a strengthened positive correlation between spring precipitation (Fig. 3-a) and yield by intensifying salt stress in the vegetative phase in the recent decade (Fig. 3-b). Meanwhile, the adverse impact of excessive precipitation after the reproductive phase has remained consistently significant, though the correlation decreased somewhat as other environmental factors were exposed to additional influence.

As SLR exacerbates long-term salinity threats, precipitation is increasingly critical in counteracting salt intrusion. In the context of climate change, SLR and salt intrusion have become increasingly severe threats in more regions, further exacerbating challenges for coastal agriculture 42,43. The significant increases in salinity (_p_ < 0.001) and sea level (_p_ < 0.01) between the first and last decade suggest that SLR is likely the leading cause of change in the region ([[NCC Fig. 4]]). Despite no statistically significant changes in precipitation, air temperature, and vapor pressure deficit, the changes in the correlation between precipitation, salinity, and yield observed in the last decade were apparent ([[NCC Fig. 4]]). This implies that SLR contributes to increased salinity stress 38,44. Precipitation, on the other hand, did not significantly decrease enough to increase salinity in the long term. Furthermore, the long-term consistency of climatic variables alongside changing salinity confirms that the transforming relationship between climate and rice yield was primarily mediated through salinity rather than direct climate impacts. This highlights the necessity for adaptive water management strategies that integrate precipitation variability with salinity monitoring to sustain rice productivity in coastal regions.

Our field-based approach provides practical insights into the complex interactions between climate factors, salinity, and rice yield. This study quantifies the temporal dynamics of multiple stressors using actual yield data, revealing the increasing vulnerability of coastal rice systems despite irrigation infrastructure. Statistical analyses employing systematic temporal adjustments provide clear insights into the changes in critical determinants of rice yield over time. However, limitations include challenges in accurately disentangling the individual contributions of multiple stressors to yield impact, necessitating more granular observational data and sophisticated modeling 45,46.

Our findings highlight that precipitation remains a critical factor for rice yield, even under well-irrigated conditions, through its indirect influence on the cultivation environment. Over time, climate change has increased the complexity of interactions among climatic variables, salinity, and rice productivity. Notably, SLR has emerged as a critical driver by intensifying salinity stress. These findings emphasize that sustainable rice production in coastal areas requires not only reliable irrigation infrastructure but also proactive integration of SLR monitoring, salinity management, and climatic variability into comprehensive water resource management. Adaptive strategies that explicitly account for these changing interactions are essential for enhancing resilience in coastal agricultural systems. Furthermore, our analytical approach provides a foundation for broader water management practices in irrigated coastal agriculture. Future efforts combining long-term, high-resolution observational data with advanced multi-stressor modeling will further refine adaptive strategies, promoting more precise and sustainable agricultural planning under changing climatic conditions.
