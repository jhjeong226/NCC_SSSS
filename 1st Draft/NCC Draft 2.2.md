# Long-Term Trends in Precipitation, Salinity, and Rice Yield

Our extended analysis from 1997 to 2023 highlights that the interactions among precipitation, reservoir salinity, and rice yield have notably evolved over time. While certain seasonal patterns remained stable, key correlations have shifted, emphasizing the increasing importance of spring precipitation and salinity dynamics in recent years compared to historical trends.

Before extending the dataset for long-term analysis, we verified its reliability by comparing the 2018–2023 period in the extended dataset with the original short-term dataset. The heatmap patterns were highly similar to those observed in [[NCC Fig. 1]]. Both datasets showed positive correlations between spring precipitation and summer salinity with rice yield, while negative correlations were observed between spring salinity and summer precipitation with rice yield. However, the correlation coefficients in the extended dataset were generally lower, and statistical significance was reduced. This discrepancy is likely due to differences in data quality. The rice yield statistics include a broader region with diverse rice varieties and irrigation practices, which may reduce sensitivity to salinity. Additionally, the lower measurement frequency of salinity data in the extended dataset weakens its monthly representativeness, particularly in May, where notable differences between the two datasets were observed. Despite these limitations, the extended dataset can capture key interaction patterns, making it a valuable alternative for examining long-term trends in this region.

The long-term dataset reveals a predominantly negative correlation between precipitation and rice yield. Specifically, precipitation during July and August showed a statistically significant negative correlation with rice yield ([[NCC Fig. 2]]-a), aligning with short-term findings. However, unlike short-term observations, no significant positive correlation was found between spring precipitation and rice yield. In the case of reservoir salinity in the long-term dataset, it has a positive correlation to rice yield in Fig. 2-b. The strongest positive correlation was observed in August and September, also consistent with short-term findings. However, unlike the short-term dataset, the negative correlation between early-season salinity and rice yield was not evident.

The seasonal connection between precipitation and salinity remained consistent over the long-term period, with higher precipitation contributing to lower salinity levels. In particular, a strong alignment between precipitation and salinity from June to November suggests interconnected impacts on yield. The influence of precipitation on salinity from January to June appeared weaker in the long-term dataset than in short-term observations.
