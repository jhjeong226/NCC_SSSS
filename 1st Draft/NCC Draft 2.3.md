# Shifting Climate Influences on Rice Yield Over Time

The impacts of spring precipitation and reservoir salinity on rice yield were not initially seen, but their influence has increased significantly in recent years. The short-term results suggested that spring precipitation and salinity influences on rice yield differed from those observed in the long-term analysis. We systematically adjusted the study window to examine these differences and analyzed the resulting correlation coefficient and statistical significance changes. [[NCC Fig. 3]]-a and [[NCC Fig. 3]]-b illustrate how the differences in the impact of variables have emerged over time, based on the year-by-year correlation analysis. The starting year for each analysis period was denoted in the x-axis, and the period for calculating correlation coefficients and statistical significance is from the starting year to 2023. This allowed us to track how the impacts of precipitation and salinity on rice yield evolved over time and assess the shifting significance of these factors in determining rice productivity.

Fig. 3-a says spring precipitation has recently emerged as a significant factor that impacts rice yield. The correlation between April precipitation and rice yield was initially negative (r = -0.32) but gradually increased over time. Starting from 2013, the correlation coefficient reached over 0.7, and the p-value fell below 0.05, indicating statistical significance. This shift confirms the emergence of a previously unidentified linkage between spring precipitation and rice yield.

The influence of salinity stress on rice yield has become increasingly evident in this region in recent years. A similar shift pattern was observed in the relationship between reservoir salinity and rice yield (Fig. 3-b). Early correlations remained weakly positive but reversed to negative beginning with the 2011 analysis period. From that point, r and p-values declined rapidly, with r reaching -0.61 in the 2016 dataset. This shift indicates that reservoir salinity, previously not a major constraint, has emerged as a significant determinant of rice productivity.

A clear threshold exists in salinity levels that significantly impact rice yield. To further investigate the threshold at which salinity affects rice yield significantly, we systematically adjusted the range of salinity values included in the correlation analysis. Fig. 3-c shows that as lower salinity values were excluded, the negative correlation between salinity and rice yield became stronger. The bars represent the range of salinity data included, while the x-axis indicates the data points used in the analysis. When salinity values below 1552 _ppm_ were excluded, the p-value dropped to 0.015, indicating a statistically significant negative correlation. This suggests that salinity must exceed a certain threshold to have a measurable impact on rice yield, and including lower salinity levels may obscure the correlation.
